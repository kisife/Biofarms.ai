Multi-Stakeholder REDD+ Implementation Project

Tropical forest conservation programme requiring structured engagement of 34+ stakeholders across government, indigenous communities, NGOs, and private sector actors for benefit-sharing agreement negotiations.

Challenge

Navigating Complex Stakeholder Landscapes

REDD+ initiatives face inherent governance complexity: divergent interests, power asymmetries, cultural and linguistic barriers, and historical distrust between communities and state actors. The project required a structured facilitation approach grounded in peer-reviewed stakeholder analysis methods.

  • High power asymmetries between indigenous groups and government
  • Divergent priorities on benefit-sharing mechanisms
  • Multi-lingual facilitation requirements (3 languages)
  • Historical conflicts over land tenure and forest access
  • Need for legally binding, consensus-based agreements
Methodology

Our Engagement Framework

01

Stakeholder Mapping

Reed et al. (2009) typology-based stakeholder identification, producing a comprehensive inventory of 34 actors across 6 categories: government, indigenous communities, NGOs, private sector, research institutions, and donors.

02

Power/Interest Analysis

Systematic mapping of each stakeholder's power (influence over decisions) and interest (stake in outcomes) using structured interviews and document analysis, producing a 2x2 matrix for prioritization.

03

Engagement Strategy

Tailored engagement approach for each quadrant: high-power/high-interest stakeholders as key players; low-power/high-interest as keep informed; high-power/low-interest as keep satisfied.

04

Facilitation Workshops

Stringer-style adaptive management workshops: 6 structured negotiation rounds with bilingual facilitation, ground rules, and interest-based bargaining rather than positional bargaining.

05

Conflict Resolution

Mediation sessions using principled negotiation (Fisher & Ury) and community-based conflict resolution mechanisms, with separate bilateral consultations for sensitive issues.

06

Agreement Documentation

Legally reviewed benefit-sharing agreements, community consent protocols, and monitoring frameworks for compliance and adaptive management.

Project Timeline

Engagement Phases

Month 1
Stakeholder Mapping & Scoping
Initial stakeholder identification using Reed typology, preliminary power/interest assessment, and relationship mapping through key informant interviews.
Month 2
Deep-dive Analysis & Strategy
Structured interviews with all 34 stakeholders, power/interest matrix validation, and tailored engagement strategy design for each quadrant.
Month 3–4
Workshops 1–3: Information Sharing & Interest Articulation
Three multi-stakeholder workshops establishing shared knowledge base, articulating individual and collective interests, and building trust through structured dialogue.
Month 5–6
Workshops 4–6: Negotiation & Agreement Drafting
Interest-based bargaining sessions, option generation, and iterative agreement drafting with bilingual legal review.
Month 7
Final Agreements & Institutionalization
Formal signing of 12 agreements, establishment of monitoring committees, and capacity building for ongoing adaptive governance.

Key Finding

The structured engagement approach achieved 12 signed agreements from 34 initially divergent stakeholders across 6 negotiation rounds. The power/interest matrix proved effective for prioritizing engagement resources: 89% of "key player" stakeholders (high power/high interest) became signatories to at least one binding agreement. Post-engagement survey indicated 78% of participants felt their interests were adequately represented, up from 34% at baseline.
