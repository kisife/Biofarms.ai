Duration: Jan 2017 – Jul 2019

Research

MSc Thesis: Constitutionality in Forest Governance — Bakossi Landscape

Institution: Technische Universitat Dresden

Program: Erasmus Mundus Joint Master's (SUTROFOR)

Duration: Aug 2023 – Feb 2024

Location: Bakossi Landscape, South West Region, Cameroon

Challenge: How do local perceptions, participation, institutional recognition, and external influence shape bottom-up forest governance in the Bakossi Landscape? How do traditional councils interact with formal conservation frameworks?

Methodology: Qualitative field research using focus groups with community members and key informant interviews with traditional council members and conservation officers. Investigated constitutionality principles and their role in shaping forest user behavior.

Outcome: Published thesis in SLUB Dresden catalogue. Found that traditional councils and indigenous institutions play critical governance roles when formally recognized within conservation frameworks. Recommendations emphasized integrating local knowledge with national policy and empowering grassroots institutions.

Key Findings
  • ✓ Emic (insider) perspectives are decisive
  • ✓ Power asymmetries shape governance outcomes
  • ✓ State recognition enables institutional effectiveness
  • ✓ Traditional councils as governance actors
  • ✓ Indigenous knowledge integration critical
  • ✓ Grassroots empowerment essential for sustainability
